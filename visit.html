History of Malaysia

The rise of the Sultanate of Malacca around the year 1400 AD is generally thought to have been the beginning of Malaysian history. At its peak, the Sultanate controlled an extensive portion of Peninsular Malaysia's and Sumatra's East Coast. Malacca's development into a significant governmental entity was made possible by its strategic importance as an intersection connecting East Asia and the Middle East. This position positioned Malacca as a significant trade hub for Southeast Asian trade, particularly in spices. Islam became the most popular religion among the people as a result of the ruler's adoption of it.

The Portuguese seized Malacca in 1511, beginning in the colonial era in the Malaya region. The Dutch then took it in 1641, and the British took over in 1824 thanks to the Anglo-Dutch Treaty. The colonization by the British lasted the longest. They centralized the administration, which had traditionally been run by the Malay kings with the help of local royalty, which enraged the people of the region. Although there were some attempts at colonial resistance, they were usually disconnected and ineffective. These initiatives were led by notable individuals including Dol Said, Tok Janggut, Datuk Bahaman, Rentap, Dato Maharajalela, Rosli Dobi, and others.

Growing numbers of people in Malaya obtained education in the local area as well as from the Middle East during the 1920s and 1930s. This educated group started promoting nationalism and used media like newspapers and magazines to spread their ideas. Some people founded organizations with the aim of removing foreign rulers and establishing their own government, such as Kesatuan Melayu Muda (KMM) and Kesatuan Melayu Singapura (KMS). As hopes for freedom rose, the Japanese invaded Malaya in late 1941, forcing yet another age of colonization. Before ending in 1945 as a result of the bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Japanese rule remained for a while.

The fight of Malayan nationalists gained new energy with the rise of Tunku Abdul Rahman. The Alliance Party, founded by Tunku, forced the British to accept the need for self-government. The London Agreement, which was signed on February 8, 1956, signaled the culmination of Malays, Chinese, and Indian unity and stated that Malaya would become independent on August 31, 1957. Upon his arrival home from London on February 20, 1956, Tunku Abdul Rahman Al-haj announced Malaya's independence. Tunku Abdul Rahman Putra Alhaj suggested the creation of a new country on May 27, 1961, by combining Malaya, Singapore, Sabah, Sarawak, and Brunei.

Religion in Malaysia

Islam

In Malaysia, most population beliefs in Islam which is the Malays. Malay culture is dominated by Islam, which is its foundation. The Arabic language, which is used for Islamic prayer and events, is the foundation of a significant amount of words in the Malay language. This is not the only language that has words from various cultures; the Malay language also contains words from English, French, Portuguese, Chinese, Dutch, Sanskrit, Tamil, and English. Islamic celebrations are practiced as part of Malay culture since Islam is so rooted in Malay society. In many everyday circumstances, the terms Muslim and Malay are the same.

Buddhism

The second most dominated religion is known as Buddhism, where it dominates the Chinese culture. As early as 200 BCE, Buddhism was introduced to the Malays and to the people of the Malay Archipelago. Approximately 30 tiny Indianized republics formed and disappeared in the Malay Peninsula, according to Chinese recorded sources. Indian traders and priests who traveled the maritime trade routes and brought Indian ideas about religion, government, and the arts are thought to be the creators of Malay-Buddhism. Hinduism and Mahayana Buddhism were among the indigenous concepts that the people of the region, particularly the royal courts, combined throughout many years, helping to define their political and cultural traditions. However, after being invaded by the Chola monarch of Tamil Nadu at the beginning of the 11th century, the Malay Kedah Kingdom renounced the Indian religion.

Christinality

Last but not least, there is a minor population who is dominated by a western origin religion which is called christinality. Early Christian presence in the Malay archipelago can be traced back to Arab Christian traders from the Arabian Peninsula who heard the gospel taught by Peter the apostle at Jerusalem (Acts 2:11), as well as those converted by Paul's ministry in Arabia (Galatians 1:17) and the evangelistic ministry of St. Thomas to the early Arabians and Nestorians from as early as the 7th century and to early Arab Christian, Persian, and Nestorian traders in Mal Penang was taken over by the British in 1786, and Malacca, which the Dutch had won in 1641, was taken over by them in 1795. The Major Seminary was founded in Penang by Thai Catholic priests in 1810.

Since 1815, the LMS has been based in Malacca and Penang. However, after 1842, when it became possible to access China, most Protestant missions stopped operating. Portuguese and French elites split the remaining Catholic leadership. Methodist ministry began in 1885, and Open Brethren ministry began in 1860. Chinese churches in Johore and congregations of immigrants in Penang, Ipoh, and Kuala Lumpur contributed to the growth of Presbyterianism. In 1932, a mission to the Sengoi natives was launched. The Charismatic Movement of the 1970s helped Pentecostalism gain more ground, but missionaries from the North American and Ceylon Pentecostal Mission (Pentecostal Church of Malaysia) had been working there since 1935.
